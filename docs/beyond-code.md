# Beyond code: the gate for any AI-assisted decision

Code review is the most legible instance of Qorum, not its boundary. A pull request comes with structure for free: a discrete artifact (the diff), a pipeline (open → review → merge), and a built-in reversibility signal (how much changed, and where). Most decisions you make *with* an AI — at the strategy table, in a hiring loop, on a pricing call — don't arrive with that structure. Qorum is a way to lend them the same one.

## The mapping

| In code | In any decision |
|---|---|
| The diff | The proposal / the AI's output you're weighing |
| Implementer | The AI (or you) producing the option |
| Reviewer | A *second* AI, told to attack it — not to improve it |
| Risk tier | How reversible, and how costly if wrong |
| Merge | Adopting the conclusion and acting on it |
| Protected paths | The irreversible domains — money, people, brand, legal |
| Human Approver | You, on the High-tier calls — and only those |

The load-bearing rule is unchanged: **an AI reviewer can clear the low-stakes calls, a human still eyeballs the middling ones, and the irreversible ones only a human ratifies.**

## Tiers, for decisions

- **🟢 Low — let the AIs run it.** Wording, framing options, organizing arguments, comparison tables, research summaries. One AI drafts, another critiques, you take the result. Cost of being wrong: you redo it.
- **🟡 Medium — review, then a human glance.** Tactics, prioritization, customer-facing drafts, hiring criteria, the plan for the week. The AI critique runs first; then you look, or you sleep on it before committing.
- **🔴 High — AI advises, human decides.** Business direction, pricing, hiring and firing, anything legal, financial, or brand that you can't take back. The AI can argue every side. It does not get to decide.

## Why not just trust your judgment per case?

Because the failure mode is specific: with a fast, fluent AI, the *low-stakes* calls and the *irreversible* ones start to feel the same — both arrive as confident paragraphs. The tier is what stops you from handling a pricing decision with the same reflex you'd give a wording tweak. You draw the line once, while you're calm — not per-decision at 11pm.

This is the inversion at the center of the whole method: the useful question isn't *what can I hand to the AI?* It's **what am I refusing to let the AI decide — and have I actually said so out loud?**

## A caution on honesty

The code gate has actually been exercised — run and corrected in the open. This generalization is the same shape pointed at a wider target — sound in structure, but you should derive your own tiers from what's genuinely reversible in your work, not import this table wholesale. The point isn't the example rows. It's the question: *which decisions have you been letting an AI make, just because it answered confidently?*
